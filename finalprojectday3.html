Birds of Ventura County

Many people love birds and birdwatching.

There are many reasons people take up birdwatching as a hobby:

  • it's a peaceful and calming hobby
  • they love watching birds
  • enjoying the outdoors and nature
  • expaning your knowledge of our feathered friends
  • to explore more of the uninhabited or natural areas of a new city or area that you are visiting

Let's explore three categories of birds in Ventura: Tree Birds, Water Birds and Birds of Prey


A.Tree birds


1. House Finches

  • Habitat:in human created buildings, lawns in urban areas. In rural areas tht are found in barns and stables.
  • Food:they eat mainly plant materials. In the wild, they eat seeds, buds and fruits.
  • Social Behavior:very social. In groups or flocks. You can see them on powerlines or the highest point of a tree.
  • Overview of Finches

2. Red Headed Acorn Woodpecker

  • Habitat:in oak and pine oak woodlands, more common in the mountains.
  • Food:acorns and insects. They drill holes in oak trees and store the seeds.
  • Social Behavior: very social. Cooperative reeding that leads to destruction of some chicks.
  • overview of Arcon Woodpecker

3. California Quail

  • Habitat:in sagebrush and chaparral and backyards with birdseed at ground level
  • Food:70% vegetarian diet; including berries, acorns and invertebrates
  • Social Behaviorvery social, living in large groups of more than 75. They are usually at the ground level and hide in brush and shrubs for protection.
  • Overview of California Quail

B.Water birds


1. Great Blue Heron

  • Habitat:in freshwater and saltwater areas. Breeding colonies are closer to water.
  • Food:will eat anything in striking distance; including fish, small reptiles, insects and small birds<./li>
  • Social Behavior:very social with elaborate courtship and pair-bonding displays. Breeding colonies can be up to several hundred pairs.
  • Overview of the Great Blue Heron

2. Great Egret

  • Habitat:in freshwater, brackish or marine wetlands.
  • Food:fresh fish, amphabians, reptiles, small birds and invertebrates
  • Social Behaviorgraceful and boyant in flight; they form monogamous pairs during the breeding season, with elaborate courtship displays. There is fierce competion among the young, and dominant chicks even stab their younger sidblings to death.
  • Overview of Great Egret

2. Brown Pelicans

  • Habitat:in estuaries and coastal marine habitats.
  • Food:small fish that live in schools near the water's surface. They dive head-first from 65 feet above. Once it hits the water, its throat pouch expands to trap fish and water.
  • Social Behaviorthey are strong swimmers and masterful fliers. Highly social, they live in colonies of several thousand pairs on small islands.
  • Overview of Brown_Pelican

C. Birds of Prey


1. Great Horned Owl

  • Habitat:they gravitate to secondary growth woodlands, swamps and agricultural areas.
  • Food:can take down birds and mammals larger than themselves.
  • Social Behavior:very active at night, at dusk and dawn. they respond to intruders with hisses, creams, bill-clappin will strike with their feet if the threat esclates. Some smaller birds like crows may harassed the Owl with loud calls and dive-bombing, chasing and pecking them.
  • Overview of the Great Horned Owl

2. Red-tailed Hawk

  • Habitat:the most common hawk in North America. They soar over open fields. They make circles to rise up thermal updrafts into higher evaluation to spot prey.
  • Food:small birds and mammals such as voles, rats, jack-rabbits and ground squirrels.)
  • Social Behavioragressive when defending nests and territories from larger birds. Mated pair remain together till death parts them.
  • Overview of Red Tailed Hawk

2. Condor

  • Habitat:they are a critically endangerded species and are at risk for extention. they live in the Western mountains and forage in the grasslands. Being heavy birds, they may have trouble taking off and launch themselves off cliffs or the highest branches to get airbone.
  • Food:Opportunistic scavangers that feed on large, dead mammals.
  • Social Behaviorlargest flying land bird in North America with a wingspan of 9 1/2 feet. They can travel 100 miles a day and live up to 60 years old in the wild.
  • Overview of Condor
